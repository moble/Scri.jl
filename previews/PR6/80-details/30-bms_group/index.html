We can write an arbitrary element of $\text{BMS}$ uniquely [13, page 173] as $(Œõ, Œ±)$ for some elements $Œõ ‚àà ‚Ñí$ and $Œ± ‚àà ùíÆ$. We interpret this as the composition of the supertranslation $Œ±$ followed by the Lorentz transformation $Œõ$.[1] The group operation is then

\[(Œõ‚ÇÇ, Œ±‚ÇÇ) (Œõ‚ÇÅ, Œ±‚ÇÅ) = (Œõ‚ÇÇ Œõ‚ÇÅ, Œ±‚ÇÅ + Œõ‚ÇÅ‚Åª¬π Œ±‚ÇÇ),\]

Decomposition of BMS

Essentially by our definition, we have already decomposed $\text{BMS}$ into the supertranslations and the Lorentz transformations. However, it can also be useful to further decompose these parts. Once we have chosen a frame $ùê≠, ùê±, ùê≤, ùê≥$ to work with, we can conventionally decompose any element of the BMS group as shown in the diagram below.

The supertranslations decompose naturally into "proper" supertranslations and ordinary spacetime translations ‚Äî the latter of which further decompose into time translations $Œ¥t$ and space translations $Œ¥x‚Éó$. In general, the supertranslations are functions on the sphere, which we decompose into spherical harmonics. If the supertranslation is a pure spacetime translation, then the only nonzero spherical harmonic modes are the $‚Ñì=0$ mode for time translations and the $‚Ñì=1$ modes for space translations. Equivalently, we can think of such translations in terms of their physical parameters $Œ¥t$ and $Œ¥x‚Éó$.

The Lorentz transformations decompose naturally into rotations $R$ and boosts $v‚Éó$. It is already convenient to represent the rotation as a quaternion, which can be constructed in numerous ways, including the generator of the rotation, the axis and angle of the rotation, the Euler angles, or the spherical coordinates and spin angle (which is essentially a different version of Euler angles). Finally, a general boost may be decomposed into a 1-D boost of rapidity $Œ∑$ in the $(Œ∏, œÜ)$ direction, and a null rotation of parameter $Œ∂$ about that direction. This is not often an intuitive decomposition, but it is important in discussions of boost weight.
